**Prepared on:** `May 20, 2026`  
**Suggested deck length:** `28 slides`  
**Suggested visual style:** bold, field-oriented, low-text, large examples, bilingual delivery where useful

## How to use this deck

- This is a speaking deck, not a reading deck.
- Each slide should carry only the strongest idea, example, or checklist.
- Use the workbook and facilitator pack alongside the presentation.
- Keep legal text light on slides and do the explanation orally.

## Detailed speaker notes

### Slide 1: From Complaint to Remedy

**Lead:** Sr Anupriya

**On slide**

- title
- names of resource persons
- one line: `Listen -> Record -> Classify -> Draft -> Submit -> Follow up -> Escalate -> Refer`

**Speaker note**

"Today is about movement. We are learning how to move a problem from fear, confusion, or rejection toward remedy."

### Slide 2: Why This Training Matters

**Lead:** Sr Anupriya

**On slide**

- community members face violence, denial, delay, and closed doors
- many cases fail because facts are not recorded well
- law becomes useful when process is understood

**Speaker note**

Use two or three examples from the district. Keep the tone grounded, not grand.

### Slide 3: Who We Are in This Room

**Lead:** Sr Anupriya

**On slide**

- PLV
- frontline worker
- youth activist
- community leader
- bridge to justice

**Interactive prompt**

Ask participants: "What do people usually come to you for?"

### Slide 4: What Participants Will Leave With

**Lead:** Sr Anupriya

**On slide**

- write a basic complaint
- write a representation
- engage police strategically
- escalate inaction
- recognize when referral is needed

**Speaker note**

Frame the training as skill-building, not information-dumping.

### Slide 5: Ground Rule: First Refusal Is Not the End

**Lead:** Sr Anupriya

**On slide**

- refusal by police
- no action by office
- document loss
- repeated delay
- next step exists

**Speaker note**

Repeat this phrase several times during the day.

### Slide 6: Rights in Everyday Life

**Lead:** Tausif

**On slide**

- rights are not abstract
- they show up in police refusal, caste violence, food denial, eviction, abuse

**Speaker note**

Use plain language. Avoid constitutional lecture tone.

### Slide 7: Articles 14, 15, 17, 19, 21 in Plain Language

**Lead:** Tausif

**On slide**

- `14`: equal treatment
- `15`: no discrimination
- `17`: no untouchability
- `19`: speech, movement, assembly, association
- `21`: life, dignity, safety

**Speaker note**

Explain `Article 21` as the most field-relevant anchor in many grassroots cases.

### Slide 8: Activity: Which Right Is Being Violated?

**Lead:** Tausif

**On slide**

- domestic violence + police indifference
- caste violence + threat
- ration or pension denied

**Activity**

Ask groups to identify:

- the right affected
- the authority involved
- the urgent next step

### Slide 9: When a Problem Becomes a Legal Issue

**Lead:** Tausif

**On slide**

- harm
- state or authority failure
- record
- relief needed

**Speaker note**

"A problem becomes legally actionable when facts, duty, and failure come together."

### Slide 10: Police, FIR, and Community Reality

### Slide 11: FIR Basics Under Section 173 BNSS

**Lead:** Nawaz

**On slide**

- cognizable offence information
- oral, written, or electronic
- reduced to writing
- signed
- entered on record

**Speaker note**

Teach FIR basics using `Section 173 of the BNSS, 2023` for new complaints.

### Slide 12: Zero FIR, Free Copy, Electronic Information

**Lead:** Nawaz

**On slide**

- zero FIR
- electronic information can be given
- signature within `3 days`
- free copy to informant or victim

**Speaker note**

Keep this strongly practical. Participants should remember rights they can immediately use.

### Slide 13: If Police Refuse: What Next?

3. write to SP

**Speaker note**

## Suggested live facilitation cues

### Cues for Sr Anupriya

- keep bringing the room back to role clarity
- emphasize ethics, patience, and community trust
- connect the technical sessions to justice outcomes

### Cues for Nawaz

- speak from real field experience
- demonstrate tone and posture with police
- show how a good complaint sounds when spoken aloud

### Cues for Nagmani

- make authority pathways concrete
- emphasize writing, receipts, enclosures, and reminders
- connect welfare failures to rights and legal strategy

### Cues for Tausif

- simplify legal concepts without flattening them
- help participants identify patterns and seriousness
- explain referral logic without making writs seem casual
