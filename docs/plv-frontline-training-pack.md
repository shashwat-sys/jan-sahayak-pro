# From Complaint to Remedy

## Interactive Training Pack for PLVs, Frontline Workers, Young Activists, and Community Leaders

**Resource persons:** Nawaz, Nagmani, Tausif  
**Supervision and overall facilitation:** Sr Anupriya  
**Suggested duration:** 1 day, `9:30 AM-6:00 PM`  
**Suggested format:** in-person, practice-heavy, bilingual where needed

## Purpose

This pack is designed for a one-day training that equips participants with basic legal action skills they can use in real community situations. The focus is not on turning participants into lawyers. The focus is on helping them:

- identify a legal wrong early
- document facts properly
- write stronger complaints and representations
- deal with police safely and strategically
- connect people to schemes and legal services
- escalate authority inaction
- recognize when a matter should be referred for litigation, including a writ under Article 226

## Core message for the day

**First refusal is not the end of the road.**

Field method to repeat in every session:

**Listen -> Record -> Classify -> Draft -> Submit -> Follow up -> Escalate -> Refer**

## Legal anchors for the training

- FIR basics should be taught using `Section 173 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023` for new complaints.
- `Article 21` should be taught as a living protection of life, dignity, safety, shelter, food, and access to justice in everyday community problems.
- `Article 32` and `Article 226` should be introduced as constitutional remedies, with emphasis that `Article 226` in the High Court is often the more practical rights-remedy route in field-linked cases.
- The role of DLSA and legal aid should be located within the `Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987`.

## Learning outcomes

By the end of the day, participants should be able to:

- distinguish complaint, FIR, representation, grievance, and writ referral
- write a basic FIR-style complaint from raw facts
- prepare a representation to a higher authority after local inaction
- identify relevant welfare or social security pathways in the same case
- maintain a chronology and document set for escalation
- assess whether a case should remain administrative, move to police escalation, or be referred for litigation support

## Session-by-session interactive material

### Session 1: Registration and expectation mapping

**Lead:** Sr Anupriya  
**Time:** `9:30-10:00`

**Activity: "Why are we here?" wall**

- Give each participant two slips.
- On the first slip, ask: "What problem brings people in your area to you most often?"
- On the second slip, ask: "What do you most want to learn today?"
- Cluster responses on a wall under `police`, `schemes`, `documents`, `violence`, `land`, `other`.

**Trainer prompt**

"Today we are not collecting random legal information. We are learning how to move a problem from pain to action."

**Output**

- a visible map of the participants' reality
- a baseline for adjusting examples during the day

### Session 2: Opening frame on the PLV role

**Lead:** Sr Anupriya  
**Time:** `10:00-10:20`

**Interactive method: "Can do / cannot do" sorting**

Read out actions and ask participants to stand left for `can do` and right for `cannot do`.

Statements:

- "Write down facts and help prepare a complaint."
- "Force the police to register a case."
- "Accompany a survivor to a police station or office."
- "Collect documents and maintain a chronology."
- "Argue independently before the High Court as if you are the advocate."
- "Connect a person to DLSA, welfare officials, or emergency services."

**Key teaching point**

PLVs and frontline workers are not substitutes for advocates. They are access-to-justice workers who document, guide, connect, escalate, and follow up.

### Session 3: Rights in real life

**Lead:** Tausif  
**Time:** `10:20-11:05`

**Interactive method: Rights-to-problem mapping**

Split participants into three groups. Give each group one situation:

- domestic violence and police indifference
- caste violence and denial of protection
- ration or pension denial despite repeated applications

Ask each group to answer:

- What happened?
- Which right is affected?
- Which authority has failed?
- What immediate relief is needed?

**Trainer board notes**

- `Article 14`: equality before law
- `Article 15`: non-discrimination
- `Article 17`: abolition of untouchability
- `Article 19`: movement, speech, assembly, association, depending on case
- `Article 21`: life, dignity, safety, survival, access

**Debrief line**

"A legal problem is not only a court matter. It often starts as a rights violation plus authority failure."

### Session 4: FIR, refusal, and strategic police engagement

**Lead:** Nawaz  
**Time:** `11:20-12:30`

**Mini-input content**

- what a cognizable offence generally means in field practice
- FIR basics under `Section 173 BNSS`
- zero FIR
- oral, written, and electronic information
- free copy to informant or victim
- refusal by police and escalation to SP
- when to document the refusal and move forward

**Interactive activity: police desk role-play**

Roles:

- complainant
- companion or PLV
- police officer
- observer

Scenario:

A woman reports repeated violence and threats by her husband and in-laws. The police officer says:

- "This is a family matter."
- "Come tomorrow."
- "Go to the local panchayat first."

Task for the PLV:

- stay calm
- present facts clearly
- insist on recording information
- request receiving or acknowledgment if available
- note name, rank, station, date, and response
- prepare next escalation step if refused

**Observer checklist**

- Did the PLV speak respectfully but firmly?
- Were facts stated clearly?
- Was urgency explained?
- Was the refusal documented?
- Was the next step identified?

**Trainer note**

Do not train confrontation as performance. Train calm persistence, safe accompaniment, documentation, and escalation.

### Session 5: Drafting Lab 1

**Lead:** Nawaz with Nagmani  
**Time:** `12:30-1:15`

**Exercise: turn story into a complaint**

Give participants a messy oral narrative and ask them to rewrite it into five parts:

- who
- what
- when
- where
- what relief is sought

**Bad draft example to critique**

"Sir, we are poor people and they are troubling us a lot. Kindly do the needful."

**Good draft features to identify**

- names and relation
- date and location
- specific acts or incidents
- prior threats or witnesses
- immediate risk
- clear request

**Output**

Each group produces one one-page complaint.

### Session 6: Representations to authorities

**Lead:** Nagmani  
**Time:** `2:00-2:45`

**Interactive activity: one problem, three representations**

Use one welfare case. Ask three groups to draft for three different authorities:

- local officer
- superior authority
- district authority

Possible authorities:

- BDO
- CO
- DM
- SP
- CDPO
- labour office
- school authority
- hospital superintendent

**Drafting formula**

- addressee
- subject line
- facts in short chronology
- previous attempts made
- documents enclosed
- relief sought
- request for time-bound action

**Trainer prompt**

"A good representation does not only complain. It creates a record."

### Session 7: Social security linkage and authority inaction

**Lead:** Nagmani  
**Time:** `2:45-3:30`

**Interactive activity: authority map**

Give the case of a widow denied ration and pension despite repeated visits. Ask groups to prepare:

- entitlement list
- authority list
- documents needed
- follow-up plan
- escalation if no action in 15 to 30 days

**Suggested issues to localize**

- ration and PDS
- pension
- disability certificate and benefits
- housing
- labour registration
- caste certificate
- victim compensation
- health scheme access

**Debrief**

Many community legal problems sit at the intersection of welfare, revenue, police, and district administration. Train participants to move across systems.

### Session 8: Escalation ladder

**Lead:** Tausif  
**Time:** `3:45-4:30`

**Interactive activity: escalation game**

Write these on separate cards:

- village or ward level authority
- police station
- superior police authority
- DLSA
- commission
- magistrate
- High Court

Read a case and ask the group to stand in the order they would escalate.

Cases:

- FIR refusal
- pension denied despite documents
- repeated caste-based threats with no police action
- demolition or eviction threat without due process

**Trainer note**

Participants should learn that escalation is not random. It is based on urgency, authority failure, evidence, and risk.

### Session 9: Strategic litigation basics

**Lead:** Tausif with Sr Anupriya  
**Time:** `4:30-5:15`

**Interactive method: "Writ-ready or not yet?"**

Read a short scenario and ask participants to vote `not yet`, `maybe`, or `refer now`.

Questions to apply:

- Is there a serious rights violation?
- Is there urgency involving life, liberty, safety, food, shelter, or dignity?
- Has the authority failed despite approach?
- Is there a pattern affecting several people?
- Are documents, dates, and previous representations available?

**Plain-language comparison card**

- `Article 32`: move Supreme Court for enforcement of fundamental rights
- `Article 226`: move High Court for fundamental rights and other legal wrongs

**Trainer boundary**

Participants should not be trained to file writs on their own. They should be trained to identify writ-worthy matters and prepare them for referral.

### Session 10: Final simulation

**Lead:** Nawaz, Nagmani, Tausif  
**Time:** `5:15-5:50`

**Task**

Each group receives one full case study and must produce:

- a fact summary
- one complaint or FIR draft
- one representation
- an authority map
- an escalation ladder
- one risk note

**Scoring criteria**

- clarity of facts
- right authority chosen
- drafting quality
- practical next steps
- attention to safety and documentation

## Case studies

### Case study 1: Domestic violence and FIR refusal

Shabana, age 28, has been facing repeated physical violence by her husband and threats from in-laws for the last six months. On `12 May`, she was beaten badly and thrown out of the house with her six-year-old daughter. She went to the police station with a neighbor, but was told to settle it at home and come back later with family elders.

**Tasks**

- draft the complaint
- identify immediate protection needs
- decide what to record about the police response
- decide next escalation steps

### Case study 2: Caste violence and police inaction

A Dalit family is prevented from drawing water from a common source. The son is beaten when he protests. The family submits a written complaint, but no FIR is registered for several days. The village atmosphere is tense and there are threats of further violence.

**Tasks**

- identify urgent safety actions
- draft representation to higher police authority
- identify statutory and administrative support avenues
- assess whether the case may need rapid legal escalation

### Case study 3: Widow denied pension and ration

Kiran Devi, a widow with no regular income, has applied for widow pension and ration restoration three times over four months. She has receipts for two applications but no order has been passed. She is borrowing food from neighbors and has a child with a disability.

**Tasks**

- identify all relevant entitlements
- draft one representation
- create a follow-up tracker
- identify when authority inaction becomes a legal issue

## Handout 1: Intake and chronology sheet

Use this format in every case:

- name of affected person
- age
- phone number
- village, ward, block, district
- problem category
- key dates
- what happened
- who is responsible
- which offices were approached
- what documents exist
- what urgent relief is needed
- safety risk level
- next action date

## Handout 2: FIR and complaint checklist

- Have you identified the person affected?
- Have you stated the incident date and place?
- Have you named the accused if known?
- Have you described the act clearly?
- Have you mentioned prior threats or repeated incidents?
- Have you included witnesses if available?
- Have you stated immediate risk or urgency?
- Have you clearly asked for registration and action?
- Have you kept one copy with yourself?
- Have you noted police refusal or delay, if any?

## Handout 4: Escalation ladder card

Use this sequence:

1. local complaint or application
2. reminder or follow-up
3. higher authority representation
4. DLSA or legal aid support
5. commission or statutory body, where relevant
6. magistrate or court-linked remedy
7. High Court referral in serious or repeated rights violations

## Handout 5: Article 32 and Article 226 quick card

- `Article 32`: constitutional remedy before the Supreme Court for enforcement of fundamental rights
- `Article 226`: writ jurisdiction of High Courts for fundamental rights and other legal wrongs
- In field practice, High Courts are often the nearer constitutional forum
- PLVs should focus on facts, chronology, records, and referrals

## Quick assessment

### Pre-test questions

- What is the difference between a complaint and an FIR?
- What should you do if police refuse to register information about a cognizable offence?
- Why is chronology important?
- What is one difference between Article 32 and Article 226?
- Name two offices apart from police that may matter in a community justice case.

### Post-test questions

- Draft a three-line subject and opening paragraph for a representation.
- Identify the next two steps after local authority inaction.
- List four facts that must appear in a strong complaint.
- Name one situation where High Court referral may be considered.

## Closing reflection

Ask each participant to complete these lines:

- "One thing I understood today is..."
- "One mistake I will avoid in future is..."
- "One real case I will act on in the next month is..."

## Trainer caution notes

- Do not over-promise outcomes to participants or communities.
- Do not reduce police engagement to aggression; emphasize safety and records.
- Do not teach constitutional remedies as shortcuts around documentation.
- Do not use outdated CrPC references for new FIR teaching without explaining the transition to BNSS.
- Localize examples, authorities, and scheme names to the district before training day.
